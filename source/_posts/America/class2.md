## Week 2 Worksheet: After the Mayflower

### I. Shifting Perspectives – From “Discovery” to “Encounter”

These questions aim to challenge the Eurocentric narrative of history and encourage students to try viewing this period from an Indigenous perspective.

#### 1. A Different Power Dynamic
The documentary shows the Wampanoag people severely weakened by disease, while the colonists were struggling on the brink of starvation and death.

> **Question:** How does this initial reality differ from the common image of the “powerful colonizer” versus the “weak Indigenous person”? How did this specific balance of power influence Massasoit’s decision to form an alliance with the colonists?

#### 2. The Other Side of Thanksgiving
The story of the “First Thanksgiving” we know emphasizes peace and sharing. However, the documentary shows that just two generations later, Metacom (King Philip) was defeated in war, and his head was displayed on a pike in Plymouth for 20 years. What new thoughts does this stark contrast provoke about the meaning of Thanksgiving? How does this illustrate the way history can be selectively remembered and told?


### Set Two: Clash of Cultures – When “Consensus” Met “Contract”

These questions delve into the misunderstandings and conflicts that arose from fundamental cultural differences, a key focus of contemporary historical research.

#### 1. Two Understandings of an Alliance
The treaty Massasoit signed with the colonists, in the Indigenous understanding, was a symbolic act of becoming one people, a kind of kinship bond. **But for the colonists**, it was likely seen more as a practical military and trade contract. How did this fundamental difference in understanding the nature of their relationship plant the seeds for future conflict?

#### 2. Who Owns the Land?
The documentary touches on land sales. For many Indigenous peoples, land was perhaps like air or water — a resource to be shared, not something that could be easily “owned” or “sold” as private property. The colonists, however, brought with them the European concept of property rights and deeds. When Massasoit and other leaders “sold” land, what fundamental differences might there have been in how each side understood what was happening?

#### 3. The Meaning of Objects
The documentary mentions that the colonists misunderstood wampum, which the Wampanoag viewed as sacred gifts or ceremonial items, as a form of “currency” and began mass-producing it for trade. How does this single misunderstanding act like a mirror, reflecting the vast differences in values and worldviews between the two societies?

### Set Three: People in History – Choices Within the Currents of Fate

These questions aim to move beyond simple labels, encouraging students to see historical figures as complex individuals making difficult decisions within specific circumstances.

#### 1. Massasoit’s Gamble
Massasoit was a leader who made a critical decision during a time of immense crisis—his people were decimated by disease and threatened by enemies. How do you evaluate his “diplomatic gamble”? Was he a successful savior of his people, or did his decisions pave the way for future disaster? Or could he have been both?

#### 2. Metacom’s War
When Metacom (King Philip) finally chose to take up arms against the colonists, was this an act of being “warlike” or an act of “forced defense”? Knowing the immense power imbalance, why do you think he launched this near-suicidal war? What did his choice signify for him as a leader of his people?

### Set Four: Echoes of History – Why Do We Retell This Story?

These questions connect the past to the present, prompting students to think about the contemporary significance of studying history.

#### 1. Who Tells the Story?
The documentary includes descendants of Indigenous people in its commentary. Why is the question of “who tells the story” so important? To what extent does watching this documentary help restore Indigenous peoples as the *subjects* of their own history?

#### 2. From History to the Present
The descendants of the Wampanoag people still live on their ancestral lands today. After learning about this history, how do you think contemporary American society and the Wampanoag tribes should approach and shape their future relationship? For example, how should we view gestures like scholarships set up by descendants of Mayflower passengers to “thank the Wampanoag for helping our Pilgrim ancestors”?

## Week 2 Key Takeaways:

1. **History is complex.** It’s not just “good guys” and “bad guys.” Both sides made choices based on their own situations. Friends can become “foes”, “Help” can bring forth burdens and dangers.
2. **Perspective matters.** The same event looks different depending on who is telling the story.
3. **Culture causes conflict.** Sometimes people fight not because one side is evil, but because they understand the world in completely different ways.
4. **The War Was a Catastrophe for Both Sides:** King Philip’s War was not a small skirmish; it was one of the bloodiest wars in American history proportional to the population. It devastated the Native population of New England and nearly destroyed the English colonial economy, proving the high cost of the cultural clash.
5. **The past is still present.** The effects of these events are still felt today by the descendants of both groups.


## Discussion Questions for Week 3 Documentary: “Trails of Tears”

#### 1. 
The episode opens by showing the Cherokee Nation’s extensive efforts to adapt to the pressure of the United States. They created a written language, a constitution modeled on the U.S. Constitution, and adopted a settled, agricultural lifestyle. Why did the Cherokee pursue this path of assimilation? What were they hoping to achieve? In what ways did this strategy ironically make their eventual removal even more tragic?

##### Answer:
The Cherokee pursued this path because they believed that by becoming "civilized" in the eyes of White Americans, they could secure their right to their ancestral lands (祖地). They hoped to prove they were a "sovereign nation" (主权国家) that could coexist with the U.S.

- The Irony: This strategy made the removal more tragic because the Cherokee had built permanent, successful lives. As the film shows, they weren't "wandering savages"; they were farmers and business owners.

- The Tragedy: When the soldiers arrived, they didn't just drive people into the woods; they forced them out of well-built homes and prosperous farms. The documentary emphasizes that the Cherokee had "beaten the Americans at their own game," yet they were still treated as outcasts.

#### 2. 
The documentary introduces two key Cherokee leaders with very different backgrounds: **John Ross** and **Major Ridge**. Based on what you saw, how did their life experiences shape their perspectives? How did these different paths lead them both to a place of leadership, and eventually, to a devastating split?

##### Answer:
- John Ross: A wealthy, highly educated leader who was only 1/8th Cherokee. His life in politics taught him to trust the "rule of law" (法治). He believed that through petitions (请愿) and the U.S. court system, the Cherokee could win. He remained a "Nationalist" until the very end.

- Major Ridge: A traditional warrior who had fought alongside Andrew Jackson. His experience in war and seeing the growing white population made him a "Pragmatist" (务实主义者).

The Split: As the state of Georgia began the "Land Lottery" (土地大乐透), Ridge realized the U.S. government would not protect them. He felt that staying meant total "annihilation" (毁灭). This led to the devastating split: Ross fought for the land, while Ridge fought to save the people.

#### 3. 
President Andrew Jackson is a central, controversial figure in this story. The documentary portrays his determination to remove the Cherokee. Do you think Jackson’s actions were driven more by a genuine belief that removal was best for both Whites and Native Americans, or by political pressure and a desire for land? What evidence from the film supports your view?

##### Answer:
The film portrays Jackson’s actions as driven primarily by political pressure and a desire for land.

- Evidence: When the Supreme Court ruled in favor of the Cherokee in Worcester v. Georgia, Jackson famously ignored it. The film suggests he was more loyal to the white settlers in Georgia (his voters) than to the Constitution.

- The Betrayal: Despite the Cherokee having helped him in past battles, Jackson viewed them as an obstacle to "Economic Progress". The discovery of gold on Cherokee land, mentioned in the film, confirms that greed for resources was a much stronger driver than any "genuine belief" in helping the Natives.

#### 4. 
The Treaty of New Echota was signed by a small faction of Cherokees, including **Major Ridge** and his son **John**, but was opposed by the vast majority, led by **Principal Chief John Ross**. Was this treaty legitimate? Why or why not? Can a treaty signed by a minority group ever be considered a binding agreement for an entire nation? Consider the immense pressure the Ridge faction was under.

##### Answer:
From a legal and moral standpoint, this treaty was illegitimate.

- Why: It was signed by a "unauthorized faction" (未经授权的小派别). John Ross had the support of over 15,000 Cherokees who signed a protest petition, but the U.S. government ignored the majority.

- Binding Agreement: A treaty signed by a minority should not be binding. However, the film shows the immense pressure the Ridge faction felt. They believed the "death of the nation" was coming if they didn't sign. As Major Ridge said while signing, "I have signed my death warrant".

#### 5. 
One reviewer of this episode noted that the story is less about a dramatic last stand and more about “how the US used political and legal machinations to dispossess Indians.” Why is it important to understand this aspect of the story? How does the “Trail of Tears” challenge the common narrative of American expansion as simply "Manifest Destiny" or the triumphant march of progress?

##### Answer:
Understanding the "political and legal machinations(手段)"  is crucial because it reveals that the removal wasn't an accident of history; it was a calculated theft.

- Challenge to the Narrative: The "Trail of Tears" proves that "Manifest Destiny" (昭昭天命) was not a "triumphant march of progress." It was a process of using laws to break laws.

- The Reality: The film shows how Georgia passed laws making it illegal for Cherokees to testify against whites in court. This "dispossession" (剥夺财产) was a systemic stripping of human rights, not a natural expansion.

#### 6. 
The documentary shows that a huge number of Cherokee died on the forced march west. However, the episode also emphasizes that the story doesn’t end with death and despair. It concludes by showing the Cherokee Nation rebuilding in Indian Territory (Oklahoma). Why was it important for the filmmakers to include this chapter of **resilience**? What does it tell us about the Cherokee people as a nation?

##### Answer:
The filmmakers included the chapter on resilience to show that the Cherokee are not just "victims" of history, but a living people.

- The Ending: After the "forced march" (强迫行军) where 4,000 people died, the survivors immediately began building schools and a new government in Oklahoma.

- Meaning: This tells us that the Cherokee Nation is defined by its will to survive. The title We Shall Remain (我们仍未消失) highlights that despite the "Trail of Tears," their culture, language, and sovereignty could not be destroyed by the U.S. government.
